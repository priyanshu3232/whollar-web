Independent, incumbent, or flanker: who actually sells you internet in Canada

Half the different-looking brands on the shelf are owned by the companies you are trying to leave.

Whollar·~6 min read

Picture the moment of triumph: after years on Bell, you switch to a cheaper, friendlier-looking brand and feel like you finally escaped.

Except, in a large share of cases, the brand you escaped to is owned by the company you escaped from. Canada's internet aisle is stocked with flankers, budget labels operated by the incumbents themselves, sitting beside a shrinking set of true independent internet providers, and from the shelf they are nearly indistinguishable.

Telling them apart is the first step to knowing whether a switch is real. Here is the map.

The short version
  • Canadian internet brands come in three kinds: incumbents who own networks, independents who rent them wholesale, and flankers, budget brands the incumbents own.
  • The flanker map: Start.ca belongs to Telus, EBOX and Distributel to Bell, VMedia and Fizz to Videotron, Oxio to Cogeco.
  • Independents' share of the market fell to roughly 6% by 2023, from about 10% in 2019, as incumbents bought them up.
  • TekSavvy remains the only major independent still standing.
  • Whollar is deliberately agnostic across all three types: in a cohort auction, ownership matters less than the bid.

The three kinds of company on the shelf

Incumbents own the physical last mile: Bell, Rogers, Telus, and the regional players like Videotron, SaskTel, and Eastlink built the cable and fibre, and everything else runs on top of it. Independents own no last-mile network; they buy regulated wholesale access to the incumbents' lines and retail service under their own brand, competing on price and terms. Flankers look exactly like independents, low prices, digital-first branding, no retail stores, but are owned and operated by an incumbent, typically to defend the low end of the market without discounting the flagship brand.

The flanker map: who owns whom

The ownership picture, as the acquisitions closed over recent years: Start.ca, the London, Ontario independent, was acquired by Telus, which also picked up Altima. Bell bought Distributel, in a deal reported at roughly $335 million, and EBOX, and has been winding parts of Distributel down. Videotron's parent Quebecor acquired VMedia, Cogeco picked up Oxio, and Fizz has always been Videotron's own digital flanker. Line those brands up beside the true independents, TekSavvy and a set of smaller regional players like CIK, Carrytel, and CanNet, and the shelf suddenly reads very differently.

Independent internet providers: the short list that is left

The consolidation shows up starkly in the numbers: independents held roughly 10% of the market in 2019 and about 6% by 2023, an estimate from BMO Capital Markets reported by The Globe and Mail, as acquisition after acquisition moved former independents under incumbent ownership. TekSavvy is the only major independent remaining, still wholesale-based nationally and even building its own SkyFi fibre, with smaller regionals like CIK, Carrytel, and CanNet rounding out the genuinely independent column. The category did not shrink because customers left it. It shrank because it was bought.

Does ownership actually matter to your bill?

Honestly: sometimes. A flanker's price can be perfectly real, and if it beats your alternatives at your address, it is a legitimate saving regardless of who owns the logo. What ownership changes is the second-order picture. A flanker exists to protect its parent's flagship pricing, so its plans are positioned carefully below the parent rather than as aggressively as a company with nothing to protect. And every independent that becomes a flanker is one fewer negotiating alternative for you to name on a retention call, which is part of why regular rates hold as firmly as they do.

Frequently asked questions

Is Start.ca an independent provider?

Not anymore. Start.ca began as a London, Ontario independent and was acquired by Telus, which also picked up Altima. It still operates as a distinct brand with its own pricing, but it is incumbent-owned, which places it in the flanker column rather than the independent one.

Does it matter who owns my internet provider?

For the connection itself, usually not: service runs over the same physical networks either way. It matters for the market around you. Flanker pricing is positioned to protect the parent's flagship rates, and every independent that gets acquired is one fewer alternative you can credibly name in a negotiation.

What is the best internet provider in Ontario?

There is no single honest answer, because it depends on which networks reach your address, what speed you need, and whether price, support, or bundling matters most to you. Independents like TekSavvy consistently anchor the low end on price; incumbents lead on plan range and bundles. The best provider for you is the best current offer at your address, which changes, and which is exactly what a competitive bid is for.

Are flanker prices real savings?

They can be. A flanker's rate is a genuine price, and if it beats your alternatives, it is a genuine saving. Just compare it against true independents on the same line before deciding, since flankers are positioned below their parent's flagship, not necessarily below the whole market.
